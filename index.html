Two camps, two worlds

The trip has a natural spine. First the high granite amphitheatre of Bavella, where the air smells of resin and the rock turns copper at dusk. Then the sea, where the same island shows its other face — posidonia meadows, white islets and fish you can meet face-to-mask.

The Field Guide · Faune & Flore Endémique

Seven islanders found nowhere else on Earth

Islands are evolution's laboratories. Cut off for millions of years, Corsica's animals and plants drifted down their own path — some shrank, some vanished, a few survive only here. Tick each one as you find it. Real naturalists keep a log; so should you.

0 / 7 species logged Tap “Log sighting” on any card when you spot it in the wild.
🐦
EndemicIUCN · Vulnerable

Corsican Nuthatch

Sitta whiteheadi
corsu: “a nuciasgiola” · ~1000–1500 m

The only bird found nowhere but Corsica — and the only bird endemic to all of metropolitan France. A tiny black-capped acrobat that runs headfirst down trunks, living and dying by the seeds of one tree: the old laricio pine.

Where: Ospedale & Bavella pine forest Size: 12 cm
For the curious

Its whole survival is tied to mature pines >100 years old — young plantations won't do. When a forest fire takes an ancient grove, it can take a century to replace. A living lesson in why "old-growth" isn't just a word.

🦌
Endemic subsp.Back from extinction

Corsican Red Deer

Cervus elaphus corsicanus
corsu: “u cerbu” · small & dark

A dwarf red deer — smaller and darker than mainland cousins. It went completely extinct in Corsica in the 1960s, then was rescued by ferrying survivors from Sardinia. Every deer on the island today descends from that rescue.

Where: Quenza enclosure & Alta Rocca Since: reintroduced 1985
For the curious

The comeback started with just 13 founder animals. That's a real-world example of a "genetic bottleneck" — ask why so few ancestors makes a conservationist nervous about inbreeding, and how the Parc naturel manages it.

🐏
Corsican race~600 left

Mouflon

Ovis gmelini musimon
corsu: “a muvra” · the island's emblem

The wild sheep of the peaks — curled horns, sure feet on bare rock. Corsica's proud silhouette against a twisted pine. Only about 600 remain on the whole island, a third of them right here in the Bavella massif.

Where: high rocky slopes, dawn/dusk Groups: 2–3 animals
For the curious

Mouflons descend from the first domestic sheep brought by Neolithic farmers ~8000 years ago, then went wild. So are they "wild" or "feral"? A genuinely debated question — perfect for a young biologist to argue both sides.

🦎
EndemicAmphibian

Corsican Brook Salamander

Euproctus montanus
corsu mountain newt · clear cold streams

A slender 10–13 cm newt that hides under stones in ice-cold torrents above 600 m. Found only on Corsica. Lift a rock in a Bavella stream (gently, and put it back!) and you might just meet one.

Where: rocky streams & springs >600 m Length: ≤ 13 cm
For the curious

It has almost no lungs as an adult and breathes largely through its skin — which is why it needs cold, oxygen-rich water. Introduced trout eat the larvae; a clean stream is its whole world.

🌲
Endemic treeFlora

Laricio Pine

Pinus nigra subsp. laricio
corsu: “u pinu laricciu” · the giant

The cathedral pillars of Bavella and Ospedale — tall, straight and ancient. Some living trees are over 500 years old. Napoleon's navy prized their masts; the nuthatch and countless insects depend on them.

Where: everywhere above 800 m Age: up to 500+ yrs
For the curious

Count the whorls of branches up a young trunk — roughly one per year — or the rings on a cut stump. You're reading a living calendar. A single old pine here may have been a sapling before Columbus sailed.

🐟
Reserve starProtected · recovering

Dusky Grouper

Epinephelus marginatus
le mérou brun · “Mérouville”, Lavezzi

A curious, heavyset fish the size of a dog that will calmly watch you snorkel. Nearly fished out of the Mediterranean, it has bounced back inside the Bonifacio reserve, where big old individuals now hold court among the rocks.

Where: Lavezzi & Cerbicale, on a boat trip Size: up to 1.2 m
For the curious

Almost every grouper is born female and later turns male — "sequential hermaphroditism." That's why the biggest fish are the old males, and why protecting them protects the whole breeding population.

🦴
ExtinctMuseum only

Prolagus, the “Rabbit-Rat”

Prolagus sardus
a lost islander · Musée de Levie

Not a species you can spot alive — a small tail-less relative of rabbits that hopped across Corsica and Sardinia for millions of years and survived until historical times, then vanished. Meet its skeleton at the Alta Rocca museum in Levie.

Where: Musée de l'Alta Rocca, Levie Fate: extinct ~Roman era+
For the curious

It shares a case with the Dame de Bonifacio — Corsica's oldest human burial, ~8500 BC. Extinction and human arrival, side by side. Ask: did people help finish off the prolagus? Islands are fragile in exactly this way.

The small kingdom · e piccule bestie

Don't just look up — look down, and after dark

The big endemics get the headlines, but the real density of life on Corsica is small: butterflies you can only find here, geckos the size of a finger, tortoises in the scrub, and a whole city in every rock pool. Here's what to hunt for once your eye is in.

EndemicStrictly protected

Corsican Swallowtail

Papilio hospiton

One of Europe's most protected butterflies — found only on Corsica and Sardinia. Big pale-yellow wings edged in black, with blue-and-red eyespots and short tails.

SPOT IT · sunny maquis & rocky slopes, May–July, near giant fennel and Corsican rue. Watch only — never catch it.

The summer sound

Cicada

Cicada orni

That relentless midday buzz is Corsica in summer — males vibrating drum-like organs to call females. They fall silent the moment it clouds over or cools.

SPOT IT · follow the sound to a tree trunk; hunt for the empty amber husk the nymph split open and climbed out of.

Smallest in Europe

Leaf-toed Gecko

Euleptes europaea

Barely the length of your finger (6–7 cm) and mostly nocturnal. It stalks insects like a tiny cat — creeping close, then pouncing. Loves granite.

SPOT IT · after dark, shine a torch on old stone walls, boulders and crags. Move slow; it freezes in the beam.

Protected

Hermann's Tortoise

Testudo hermanni

The tortoise of the Corsican scrub, plodding through the maquis grazing on flowers. Once common, now getting rare — protected by law across Europe.

SPOT IT · warm mornings on tracks through the maquis. Photograph it, note the spot — never pick it up or take it home.

Critically endangered

Fan Mussel

Pinna nobilis

The Mediterranean's giant shell — up to a metre tall, standing point-down in the seagrass. A parasite has killed nearly all of them since 2016, so a living one is a real rarity.

SPOT IT · in Posidonia meadows while snorkelling; you may find old empty shells. If you meet a live one, keep your distance.

Camouflage master

Mantis & Stick Insects

Mantis religiosa

Masters of disguise in the dry grass — a mantis holds itself as still as a twig, then strikes in a blink. Stick insects take invisibility even further.

SPOT IT · dry grass stems and shrubs in late summer. Look for the alien head that swivels to watch you.

Hidden ambush

Ant-lion

Myrmeleon sp.

Those little conical pits in dry, sheltered sand are traps. A fierce, buried larva waits at the bottom for an ant to slip down the crumbling slope.

TRY IT · tip a single grain of sand down the funnel and watch the bottom twitch as the hunter reacts.

Rock-pool city

Sea Urchins & Co.

Paracentrotus lividus

Purple urchins, hermit crabs, blennies and starfish share the sunlit shallows — an entire city living under 30 cm of water, if you slow down enough to see it.

SPOT IT · rocky shallows on a calm day. Mind the urchin spines — look, don't grab.

Turn the holiday into fieldwork

The young naturalist's notebook

A curious, science-minded mind gets ten times more from Corsica than a camera roll. Here are real methods a field biologist uses — small enough to do on a trail or a beach, real enough to write up for school.

01

Learn the mountain's soundtrack

Most forest animals are heard before seen. Sit still for five silent minutes and just listen — a discipline birdwatchers call a "sit spot."

Try thisThe Corsican nuthatch gives a nasal, repeated “wee-wee-wee.” Learn it, then find the bird. Ears first, eyes second.

02

Read a tree's age

You don't need to cut a pine to date it. Count the annual whorls of branches up a young trunk, or the rings on any fallen log or cut stump.

Try thisFind the oldest laricio you can and estimate its birth year. Was it alive during the French Revolution? Almost certainly.

03

Sketch, don't just snap

Darwin and every great naturalist drew what they saw. The act of sketching forces you to notice — how many toes, where the stripe sits, how the leaf attaches.

Try thisKeep one small notebook. Date, place, weather, sketch. That format is exactly a real scientific field log.

04

Investigate the beach's brown balls

Those fibrous balls on Palombaggia (égagropiles) are shredded Posidonia seagrass, rolled by the waves. Evidence of a whole underwater meadow offshore.

Try thisPull one apart. Those fibres are from a plant, not seaweed — Posidonia is a true flowering plant that lives in the sea. Why is that remarkable?

05

Think like an island

Why does Corsica have its own deer, its own bird, its own newt? Because islands run evolution as a closed experiment — this is "island biogeography."

Try thisFor each endemic you log, ask the same question: what did isolation change — size, colour, diet? Build the pattern yourself.

06

Leave no trace — the scientist's oath

Every rock you lift in a stream, you replace. Every animal, you watch from a distance. Nothing collected, nothing fed, nothing left behind.

Try thisPhotograph and log instead of collecting. Upload sightings to iNaturalist — your holiday data becomes real citizen science.

07

Go “mothing” after dark

A warm, still night turns on a light show. Hang a pale sheet, shine a torch on it, and wait — moths, beetles and lacewings appear out of the dark.

Try thisBest on a muggy, moonless night. Photograph every visitor; some Corsican moths are jaw-droppers, and no two nights are the same.

08

Master the rock pool

The shallows are a nursery you can study standing up. Move slowly, keep your shadow off the water, and let it settle before you look.

Try thisScoop into a clear tub, watch for two minutes, then tip it gently back. Catch, observe, release — the whole cycle, every time.

09

Read the cicada thermometer

Cicadas only crank up their song when it's hot — roughly above 22 °C. The fuller the chorus, the warmer the air. The insect is a living instrument.

Try thisTrack how the buzz swells through the morning as the sun climbs. You are literally listening to the temperature rise.

Before you go · le carnet pratique
🌅

Chase the light

Bavella's granite glows copper at dawn and dusk, and beaches are emptiest then. Early starts beat both heat and crowds.

💧

Carry water & sun

The rocky trails are shadeless and Mediterranean sun is fierce. Two litres each, a hat, and reef-safe sunscreen for snorkel days.

🥾

Grippy shoes

Wet granite is slick. Proper trail shoes for hikes; old trainers or reef shoes you can swim in for canyons and coves.

🛡️

Guides & ages

Canyoning and via ferrata are booked with certified guides who set the real age and height rules on the day. Confirm ahead.

🔭

Pack the tools

Binoculars for the peaks, a mask for the sea, a snorkel camera, and one small notebook. That's the whole naturalist's kit.

🌿

Respect the reserve

Inside the Bouches de Bonifacio: no touching, feeding, or taking. It's protected precisely so you can meet a wild grouper.

Come down the mountain slowly. The island keeps its best secrets for the ones who stop, look, and log.
